SLIDE 1 — TITLE

A Mental Model for AI

For software engineers who already use it but haven't examined how.

Speaker notes: Don't preamble. Skip "what is AI." This audience uses it daily; you're here to make their implicit model visible.

SLIDE 2 — PART 1 · THE HOOK

You already have a mental model of AI.

You just haven't examined it.

Speaker notes: Most engineers absorbed a model from Twitter, podcasts, vendor pitches. That implicit model is steering every prompt and every prompt outcome. The talk is about making it conscious — not adopting one from scratch.

SLIDE 3 — PART 1 · THE MECHANISM

Mental models are compressed representations.

They let you run cheap simulations of "what if I did X?" without doing X.

Working memory is bounded. Compact models leave room for more options — which is where novel ideas come from.

Speaker notes: This is why intuition works. Experts see patterns novices don't because their compression is denser. The point isn't a complete model — it's a compact, mostly-correct one that frees cognitive room.

SLIDE 4 — PART 1 · THE RISK

Unconscious models for things you interact with daily are the highest-risk category.

Confidently-wrong  >  no model  >  examined-and-imperfect

Speaker notes: Bad models compound silently — every interaction reinforces them. AI and your IDE are in this category. Engineers who modeled async as parallelism shipped buggy code for years. The fix isn't getting the model perfectly right; it's keeping it conscious and refinable.

SLIDE 5 — PART 1 · MODELS THAT WORKED BECAUSE THEY WERE SIMPLE

Three shifts. None complete. All transformative.

The shift What it unlocked
"The universe is mysterious and unpredictable"  →  "The universe follows mathematical patterns" The entire scientific revolution
"Diseases come from bad air or imbalance"  →  "Diseases are caused by invisible organisms" Modern medicine, sterile surgery, public sanitation
"A program is what the computer runs"  →  "A program can read a text file and treat that as code" Compilers, interpreters, linters, formatters, IDEs, eval, JIT, code generators, AI code completion
Speaker notes: The same noun — a program — shifts roles between left and right of the third row. Before: a program is the thing being run. After: a program is the thing doing the reading. That subject-flip is where every tool on your machine comes from. Linters, compilers, code formatters, your AI autocomplete — all of them are programs reading text and treating it as code. We're going to do the same exercise for AI: take the simple, mostly-correct version that unlocks the most useful behavior.

SLIDE 7 — PART 2 · WHAT MODEL ARE YOU ALREADY USING?

Models you might already be using

Model Predicts well Misses
Autocomplete on steroids Text completion, simple code suggestions Agency, tool use, multi-step reasoning
Junior intern Mistakes, needs supervision, benefits from feedback Vast knowledge breadth; isn't a brainstorming partner by default; supplying the what + why improves AI's output, while a junior might be overwhelmed by it
Stochastic parrot Hallucination, surface mimicry, no understanding Actual usefulness in real workflows

Each captures a fragment. None alone is enough.

Speaker notes: "Show of hands — which one are you carrying?" Walk through each. Don't dunk on them — each captures a real fragment. Autocomplete predicts continuation. Intern predicts the supervision burden. Parrot predicts hallucinations. Each is partial. The intern lens hurts the most for senior engineers — you brainstorm with peers, you give tasks to interns; treating AI as an intern caps you at small delegations. Counterintuitive: the what + why discipline that helps AI is the opposite of how you'd onboard a junior. Managers good at gradual context-building for humans will be worse at directing AI.

SLIDE 8 — PART 2 · WHERE THIS LANDS

One artifact. Two bounds.
You live in between.

  • Upper bound — a smart, knowledgeable human who doesn't know what you want or why, and has no body. Where the field is heading.
  • Lower bound — a text-to-text parser with tool calls. What you must assume when reliability matters and you're out of the loop.

The skill is calibration — based on how tight your verification loop is.

Speaker notes: The candidates on the previous slide were partial views — autocomplete is a primitive lower bound, intern is a primitive upper bound, parrot is the pessimistic floor-only view. These two bounds subsume what's right in each and discard what's wrong. You don't pick one; you operate in between, calibrating by how tight your verification loop is. Tight loop (you check each output) → operate near the upper bound. No loop (autonomous in production) → operate near the lower bound.

SLIDE 9 — PART 2 · UPPER BOUND

Upper bound — a smart, knowledgeable human who doesn't know what you want or why, and has no body.

What the field is building toward. What AI approaches under tight verification loops.

Operate near this bound when you can verify each output before acting on it: brainstorming, debugging interactively, drafting RFCs.

Give it what + why. Hand it tools (search, code execution) when accuracy matters.

Permanent property — even at the upper bound:

It cannot know your exact what and why. Articulating intent is a forever-skill — it doesn't shrink with model capability.

Caveats — how the current upper bound falls short:

  • Demand pushback. Sycophancy is the default.
  • Verify facts. It confidently invents.
  • Force clarifying questions. It plows ahead silently.
Speaker notes: Two layers. (1) The caveats aren't permanent — they're how the current approximation falls short of the upper-bound ideal. As models improve, these caveats shrink. (2) The what + why property is structural — it doesn't shrink with model capability. No matter how smart AI gets, you carry the intent. That's why intent articulation is a forever-skill, not a transitional one.

SLIDE 10 — PART 2 · LOWER BOUND

Lower bound — a text-to-text parser with tool calls (tool calls are also text).

What you must assume when reliability matters and you're out of the loop.

Operate near this bound when you can't verify each output: production systems, autonomous workflows, anything running while you're absent.

Useful insights — even at the lower bound:

  • You can give it tools to make non-deterministic, abstract decisions while you're absent. The floor is already useful — you don't need AGI to ship something autonomous.
  • You don't need the biggest, best, or most expensive model. A small, cheap, even local model that understands your use case and tools (e.g., parsing grep output to act as an intelligent file searcher) is enough to ship and stay afloat for a while.

Caveat — three dials:

  • Narrower input (less ambiguity, fewer ways to misinterpret)
  • Narrower output space (schemas, formats, fixed options)
  • Shorter output (less room to drift)

All three compound.

Speaker notes: Two implications of treating it as just a parser. (1) Agentic: floor-level AI plus tools plus constraints runs abstract decisions in production while you're absent — don't wait for the upper bound. (2) Cost: you stop chasing frontier models. A small/cheap/local model that understands your tools (e.g., one that runs grep and parses output) is often enough. The race for biggest model is a smart-human-mode habit; parser mode rewards "smallest model that gets the job done." Both ends of the parser matter — vague input + tight schema still produces confident-but-wrong answers; precise input + open essay format drifts.

SLIDE 11 — PART 2 · CALIBRATE, THEN RECALIBRATE

Calibrate by how tight your verification loop is.

Tight loop (you check each output) → operate near the upper bound.
No loop (autonomous in production) → operate near the lower bound.

When something surprises you, you've miscalibrated. Recalibrate.

You assumed… …and surprised that Recalibrate toward
Upper bound AI forgot last week's chat Lower bound — pipe memory in explicitly
Lower bound 20 schema rules made output worse Upper bound — explain the why
Two action insights to carry alongside:
  • Even at the upper bound, AI can't know your exact what + why — articulate intent, always.
  • Even at the lower bound, tools + constraints make AI autonomously useful — build agentic systems now, don't wait.
Speaker notes: Notice the surprise, name which bound you were assuming, slide toward the other. The two action insights below the table are the takeaways the audience should leave with — they hold whether the upper bound is fully achieved or not, and they hold whether the lower bound is the floor or rising.

SLIDE 12 — PART 2 · WHAT "CONTEXT" ACTUALLY MEANS

Context = what you want + why you want it.

Everything else (state, constraints, conventions, taste, examples) is a sub-question of one of those two.

Even at the upper bound, AI cannot know these. You carry the intent — forever.

Speaker notes: This is the operative definition. When anyone says "give it more context" — what + why is what they mean. The connection back to the upper-bound permanence: whatever AI becomes, the what + why gap stays. Intent articulation is the permanent skill — Part 3 will treat it as the core engineering hire-for skill.

SLIDE 13 — PUSHBACK PAUSE #2 · ~3 MIN

Pushback?

Speaker notes: ~3 min. Most-likely pushback: "but X failure mode breaks your model." Steelman it, name which lens it lives under, refine in real time. This is the comparative-framing payoff.

SLIDE 14 — PART 3 · WORK

What changes about your work.

  • Intent articulation becomes a core engineering skill. The what + why is the work; coding speed matters less.
  • Pushback discipline becomes hygiene. Sycophancy is the default failure mode; you must demand challenge.
  • Use cases expand beyond coding. Architecture, RFC review, postmortems, comms drafts, code review, career thinking, strategy — anywhere a smart-human collaborator would help.
Speaker notes: The "more use cases" expansion is the practical lift. Most senior engineers cap AI at coding; the smart-human lens predicts it's useful anywhere brainstorming is. The bottleneck has moved — writing speed of code matters less; specifying intent clearly matters more.

SLIDE 15 — PART 3 · FIELD

What changes about the field.

  • Hiring profile shifts toward specification + verification.
  • Frameworks go schema-first, verification-first.
  • Comp curves bend for engineers who can drive AI well.
Speaker notes: Brief slide. These are second-order consequences for the audience to chew on, not predictions to defend in detail.

SLIDE 16 — PART 3 · THE GAP CLOSING

The maintenance schedule — what to watch for.

Signals that the upper bound's caveats are dissolving — i.e. the gap between bounds is closing.

  • If AI starts asking good clarifying questions by default → upper bound's first caveat dissolves.
  • If persistent memory across sessions becomes default → upper bound gets closer to literal.
  • If sycophancy is engineered out → user-side enforcement burden drops dramatically.

But the structural what + why limit at the upper bound stays. You always carry the intent.

Speaker notes: Most talks skip this slide. Including it is the comparative-framing move — you're not selling a thesis; you're handing them a tool with a service interval. The gap-closing framing also dignifies the audience's skepticism: yes, things will change, here's how to update the model. The permanence of what + why is the durable through-line — it's true today, true at AGI, true after.

SLIDE 17 — PART 3 · MONDAY MORNING

Pick one. Do it this week.

  • Audit your implicit model. Spend 10 minutes writing what you currently assume about AI before your next prompt.
  • Pre-mortem your next session. Write the what, the why, what you're not giving, and your prediction for the failure mode. Score yourself afterward.
  • Track sycophancy for one week. Every time AI agrees with you, ask "would a competent skeptic have agreed?" Count the gap.
Speaker notes: Pre-mortem is the strongest — smallest action that exercises the whole model in one session. Three is the cap; don't read all six options from your private list.

SLIDE 18 — CLOSE

The best working model I've found so far.

Push back. Refine. Replace it when it breaks.

Speaker notes: End on the comparative frame. The model isn't the point; keeping a conscious model is the point. Invite refinement explicitly — these are curious seniors, they'll take you up on it.
