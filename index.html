Part I · The Mechanism
03

Mental models are compressed representations.

They let you run cheap simulations of "what if I did X?" without doing X.

Working memory is bounded. Compact models leave room for more options — which is where novel ideas come from.

03 · 18
03 · The Mechanism
This is why intuition works. Experts see patterns novices don't because their compression is denser. The point isn't a complete model — it's a compact, mostly-correct one that frees cognitive room.

Part I · The Risk

Unconscious models for things you interact with daily are the highest-risk category.

— Hierarchy of harm —

Bad models compound silently — every interaction reinforces them. AI and your IDE are in this category. Engineers who modeled async as parallelism shipped buggy code for years. The fix isn't getting the model perfectly right; it's keeping it conscious and refinable.

Part II · Upper Bound

— Upper bound —

A smart, knowledgeable human who doesn't know what you want or why, and has no body.

What the field is building toward. What AI approaches under tight verification loops.

Operate near this bound when you can verify each output before acting on it — brainstorming, debugging interactively, drafting RFCs.

Give it what + why. Hand it tools when accuracy matters — same as a human with a reference book.

Permanent property Even at the upper bound, AI cannot know your exact what and why. Articulating intent is a forever-skill — it doesn't shrink with model capability.
How the current upper bound falls short
  • Demand pushback. Sycophancy is the default.
  • Verify facts. It confidently invents.
  • Force clarifying questions. It plows ahead silently.
09 · 18
09 · Upper Bound
Two layers. (1) The caveats aren't permanent — they're how the current approximation falls short. As models improve, these shrink. (2) The what + why property is structural — it doesn't shrink with model capability. No matter how smart AI gets, you carry the intent.
10
Part II · Lower Bound

— Lower bound —

A text-to-text parser with tool calls (tool calls are also text).

What you must assume when reliability matters and you're out of the loop.

Operate near this bound when you can't verify each output — production systems, autonomous workflows, anything running while you're absent.

Useful insights — even at the floor
  • Give it tools to make non-deterministic, abstract decisions while you're absent. The floor is already useful.
  • You don't need the biggest, best, most expensive model. A small, cheap, even local model that understands your tools (e.g. parsing grep output) is enough to ship.
Caveat — three dials
  • Narrower input · less ambiguity.
  • Narrower output space · schemas, formats.
  • Shorter output · less drift.

All three compound.

10 · 18
10 · Lower Bound
Two implications of treating it as just a parser. (1) Agentic: floor-level AI plus tools plus constraints runs abstract decisions in production. (2) Cost: you stop chasing frontier models. The race for biggest model is a smart-human-mode habit; parser mode rewards "smallest model that gets the job done."

— The skill is calibration —

Calibrate by how tight your verification loop is.

Tight loop (you check each output) → operate near the upper bound.   No loop (autonomous in production) → operate near the lower bound.

When something surprises you, you've miscalibrated. Recalibrate.

— The operative definition —

Context = what you want
+ why you want it.

Everything else — state, constraints, conventions, taste, examples — is a sub-question of one of those two.

Even at the upper bound, AI cannot know these.
You carry the intent — forever.

Part III · Work

— Consequences for your work —

What changes about your work.

  • i
    Intent articulation becomes a core engineering skill. The what + why is the work; coding speed matters less.
  • ii
    Pushback discipline becomes hygiene. Sycophancy is the default failure mode; you must demand challenge.
  • iii
    Use cases expand beyond coding. Architecture, RFC review, postmortems, comms drafts, code review, career thinking, strategy — anywhere a smart-human collaborator would help.
14 · 18
14 · Your Work
The "more use cases" expansion is the practical lift. Most senior engineers cap AI at coding; the smart-human lens predicts it's useful anywhere brainstorming is.
15
Part III · Field

— Second-order consequences —

What changes about the field.

  • i
    Hiring profile shifts toward specification + verification. Not "writes code"; "specifies intent and verifies output."
  • ii
    Frameworks go schema-first, verification-first. The next generation of tooling assumes structured I/O and runtime checks.
  • iii
    Comp curves bend for engineers who can drive AI well. The premium moves from "fast coder" to "clear specifier."

Part III · Maintenance

— The gap closing —

The maintenance schedule.

Signals that the upper bound's caveats are dissolving — the gap between bounds closing.

  • ·
    If AI starts asking good clarifying questions by default → upper bound's first caveat dissolves.
  • ·
    If persistent memory across sessions becomes default → upper bound gets closer to literal.
  • ·
    If sycophancy is engineered out → user-side enforcement burden drops.

But the structural what + why limit at the upper bound stays.
You always carry the intent.

Part III · Monday Morning

— This week —

Pick one. Do it this week.

  • 01
    Audit your implicit model. Spend 10 minutes writing what you currently assume about AI before your next prompt.
  • 02
    Pre-mortem your next session. Write the what, the why, what you're not giving, and your prediction for the failure mode. Score yourself afterward.
  • 03
    Track sycophancy for one week. Every time AI agrees with you, ask "would a competent skeptic have agreed?" Count the gap.
