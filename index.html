02 · The Premise
Every prompt is steered by an implicit model absorbed from Twitter, vendors, podcasts. The category that compounds silently is the one you use daily — AI lives in that category. Bad models reinforce themselves every interaction because the predictions are never checked. The fix isn't getting the model perfectly right; it's keeping it conscious and refinable.

Part I · The Mechanism

Mental models are simulation devices.

They let you ask "what if I did X?" without doing X.

Compact-and-mostly-right beats complete-and-overwhelming.

A chess master reconstructs a mid-game board at a glance; a novice stares piece-by-piece. Same eyes, different compression.

03 · 14
03 · The Mechanism
This is why intuition works. Experts see patterns novices don't because their compression is denser. The point isn't a complete model — it's a compact, mostly-correct one that frees cognitive room. Chase & Simon 1973 is the citation; show random-board condition to make the point that compression is domain-specific, not raw memory.
Part I · Three Shifts

Models that worked because they were simple.

None complete. All transformative. You're allowed to use a simple one.

Part II · Where This Lands

One artifact.
Two bounds.
You live in between.

Upper bound

A smart, knowledgeable human.

Doesn't know your intent. Forgets between sessions. Where the field is heading.

Lower bound

A text-to-text parser.

Rearranges what you put in. What to assume when you're out of the loop.

The skill is calibration — based on how closely you're watching.

08 · 14
08 · Bounds
The four candidates were partial views — autocomplete is a primitive lower bound, intern is a primitive upper bound, parrot is the pessimistic floor-only view, genie is the optimistic upper-bound that forgets you still carry intent. These two bounds subsume what's right and discard what's wrong. The "smart human" is a memory aid — flag the three deltas (forgets between sessions, can't read intent, parallelizable) so the analogy doesn't leak.
Part II · Calibrate

— The skill is calibration —

Calibrate by how closely you're watching.

Tight watch → smart person: give it what + why, hand it tools.   No watch → parser: narrow inputs, narrow outputs, keep them short.

Surprise = miscalibration. Slide bounds.

Operative definition Context = what you want + why you want it. Everything else is a sub-question of one of those two.
09 · 14
09 · Calibrate
Notice the surprise, name which bound you were assuming, slide toward the other. The "context = what+why" line is the operative definition — when anyone says "give it more context," what + why is what they mean. Bring up METR's 2025 RCT here if it lands: developers predicted +20% speedup, measured −19%. Every participant ran an uncalibrated model until measurement forced contact.

Part III · Today

— What the model unlocks —

Three things you can act on today.

  • i
    Ship at the floor. Small, cheap, or local + good tools + tight constraints already do production work. Stop waiting for the frontier.
  • ii
    Expand the surface. Anywhere a smart-human collaborator helps, AI does. Architecture, RFC review, postmortems, hiring rubrics, vendor evaluation, strategy.
  • iii
    Hire and promote for specification + verification. "Writes code fast" is no longer the premium. "Specifies intent clearly and verifies output rigorously" is.
11 · 14
11 · Today
Three direct consequences of the two-bounds model. (i) lower-bound implication: constraint substitutes for capability. Anecdote: smallest Gemma + a project-graph build of a codebase answered architecture questions correctly. (ii) upper-bound implication: smart-human lens predicts AI is useful wherever a smart peer would help. Anecdotes: Socratic physics tutoring; Rokomari founder market-share brainstorming. (iii) field-level shift derived from "intent articulation is the forever-skill" — changes who you hire, promote, develop.
Part III · Forever

— The one thing that doesn't shrink —

Articulating what + why is the forever-skill.

You write simple UIs because a teacher mentioned his grandma struggles with phones. Not in your repo. Not on your screen. But in every interface you design.

Your code has feature flags because a boss said over coffee that we don't ship on Fridays. AI reads your code. It can't read the coffee break.

Permanent property Tacit context is structurally unobservable. Memory and screen-watching don't reach what you couldn't write down if asked.
For people building AI products Evals are how what + why survives between sessions.
  • Concrete input/output examples: good vs. bad.
  • Without them, intent decays into vibes.
12 · 14
12 · Forever
The grandma/coffee-break example is the strongest single argument in the talk — tell it slowly. Polanyi 1966 ("we know more than we can tell") is the citation if anyone presses. Evals: cite Anthropic's Demystifying Evals for AI Agents and the OpenAI Cookbook's eval-driven design entry. METR's 2025 RCT (devs wrong about own productivity by ~40 percentage points) is the strongest argument for evals — self-assessment is unreliable.
Part III · In Practice

— Three prompts, same goal —

The forever-skill, in three prompts.

Same goal. The version that looks worse carries what + why.

“Give me the code for printing a flower on the terminal.”
→
“amake ekta choto python script dao ja terminal e ekta golap ful print korbe, dekhte impressive hobe, ami demo te dekhabo.”
No what or why → parser bound, generic flower. Rose + demo → smart-person bound makes the unstated choices.
“Write 5 pytest tests. Cover empty, null, unicode, boundary.”
→
“I want to write 5 python tests for this project. Tell me in English the cases that are usually missed.”
… then → “With the cases you identified, write the tests.”
Prose first → smart-person reasons. Spec second → parser writes. One-shot collapses to parser only — you get the four buckets you named, none you didn't.
“Design a sign-up form with email, password, and confirm password.”
→
“Design a sign-up form. Our users skew 50+ and rural, many are first-time smartphone users on cheap Android phones with patchy 3G.”
Left is a complete spec on its face — same form anyone gets. Right carries the constraints that live outside the repo. The forever-skill.
13 · 14
13 · In Practice
Read the “looks better” prompt aloud first; pause; read the “actually better” version. For #1, ask the room which they'd pick before revealing the reasoning column — the polished English looks more professional, and that's the trap. For #2, emphasise that the one-shot version gives back exactly the buckets you named: the cases you didn't think of stay invisible. For #3, this is the grandma anecdote from the previous slide made concrete. The abstract what + why claim becomes prompts people will write tomorrow morning.

— What's wrong now. What dissolves. What doesn't. —

Hold the model loosely.

  • ·
    AI defaults to agreement (sycophancy) and to silent action on ambiguity. Pushback and clarifying questions must be demanded.
  • ·
    Hallucination, sycophancy, silent plowing — these dissolve with model capability. Plan for it.
  • ·
    The intent gap doesn't dissolve. That one is structural. You carry it forever.

The model itself is provisional.
Push back. Refine. Replace it when it breaks.
