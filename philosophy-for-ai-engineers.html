§ VI·Hallucination · 1 of 4 06 / 19
VI On Bullshit — the master concept Harry Frankfurt, On Bullshit1986

An LLM is not a liar. It is a bullshitter, in Frankfurt's technical sense.

The argument A liar knows the truth and says the opposite — they care about truth, just inversely. A bullshitter doesn't care about truth at all; they say whatever serves their purpose, indifferent to truth or falsity. Frankfurt's strong claim: bullshit is worse than lying, because the bullshitter has no orientation toward truth whatsoever. An LLM is structurally a bullshitter — not by malice, by construction.
 Common sense

"It's a knowledgeable assistant that occasionally makes mistakes."

This frame assumes the model is trying to be truthful and sometimes failing. It isn't trying. There is no trying.

 The move

Treat every output as bullshit until externally verified.

Your job is to supply truth-orientation, because the model has none. RAG, search, code execution, citation lookup — not tricks. They are the only sources of truth-orientation in the loop.

VI · Frankfurt ← →
The master concept of the talk. If the audience leaves with one frame change, this is it. "Bullshit" is technical here, not an insult — Frankfurt uses it precisely. Restate that so it doesn't read as cheap.
§ VII·Hallucination · 2 of 4 07 / 19
VII Why citations and APIs hallucinate specifically Gottlob Frege, Über Sinn und Bedeutung1892

Form is what's reliable. Reference is what isn't.

The argument Sense (Sinn) is the conceptual content — what kind of thing a word picks out. Reference (Bedeutung) is the actual thing in the world. "The current king of France" has perfectly good sense and no reference. LLMs operate almost entirely at the sense level. A hallucinated citation has the sense of a citation in this domain — right author shape, right journal, right year — with no reference to any actual paper.
 Common sense

A well-formed citation is evidence of a real referent.

The opposite is true. Form is exactly what's reliable in LLM output, and reference is exactly what isn't.

 The move

Treat every referring expression as sense without reference until checked.

Citations: look them up. APIs: try them. Versions: verify. The verification step is the reference relation — without it, you have form without anchor.

VII · Frege ← →
Explains why hallucinations cluster around specific kinds of content: anything that names a particular real-world entity. The model is fine at conceptual answers because those live entirely at the sense level. Hallucination shows up where reference is required.
§ VIII·Hallucination · 3 of 4 08 / 19
VIII Right opinion vs knowledge Plato, Meno~ 380 BCE

Even when it's right, it isn't knowledge.

The argument Plato distinguishes right opinion (true belief without justification) from knowledge — true belief with logos, an account. The road-to-Larissa example: a man who happens to know the right way gives correct directions — but if he can't tell you why, you're depending on luck. LLMs produce right opinion. Even when correct, output has no traceable justification. Tokens like that follow tokens like the prompt.
 Common sense

"It's been right ten times in a row, so I trust it."

Ten right opinions don't add up to knowledge. They are ten lucky bets. The eleventh prompt is a fresh roll.

 The move

Demand the logos.

After any non-trivial answer, ask: "What would have to be true for this to be wrong, and what's the strongest evidence against it?" Answers that fold immediately are right opinion at best. Answers that hold up are closer to knowledge.

VIII · Plato ← →
Larissa example is from the Meno, not the Theaetetus — minor philological note. Main move: trustworthiness comes from justification structure, not past correctness.
§ IX·Hallucination · 4 of 4 09 / 19
IX Confidence is not calibration David Hume, A Treatise of Human Nature1739

Confidence is a stylistic feature. Not a signal.

The argument All inductive inference — projecting past patterns onto future cases — is unjustified by reason alone. We project frequencies because of "custom and habit," not logic. LLMs are pure inductive engines. Their expressed confidence is inherited from confident-sounding text in the training data, not a calibrated internal probability. Tone and accuracy are uncorrelated except by accident.
 Common sense

Confident output gets more trust; hedged output gets less.

Reading model tone as a calibration signal. The correlation is essentially zero.

 The move

Discount stated confidence to roughly zero.

Build confidence externally: multiple generations, stability checks, ground-truth comparison. Ask for evidence, not for confidence. "What's the evidence?" triggers a different generation pattern than "are you sure?"

IX · Hume ← →
The "are you sure?" vs "what's the evidence?" distinction is a small concrete shift the audience can do tomorrow. Both prompts well-formed; one produces grounding behavior, the other produces more confident-sounding tokens.
§ X·Instruction · 1 of 4 10 / 19
X No prompt uniquely determines its output Wittgenstein, Philosophical Investigations § 2011953

No spec, however clear, can uniquely determine its application.

The argument Show someone 2, 4, 6, 8 and say "continue the pattern." They write 10, 12, 14. But "add 2 each step until step 100, then jump to 1000" also fits. Nothing in the rule itself rules out the second reading. What makes the first reading obvious is shared background — what Wittgenstein calls a "form of life." The model has a different form of life. It fills the gaps with training-distribution averages.
 Common sense

"If the spec is clear enough, output will match."

Unreachable in principle — not because specs are sloppy, but because no spec determines its application uniquely.

 The move

Stop writing longer prompts. Write prompts with concrete examples.

Two or three (input → desired output) pairs do more than two paragraphs. This is the philosophical reason few-shot prompting works.

X · Wittgenstein I ← →
The 2-4-6-8 example is the cleanest version. Engineers tend to think they can tighten the spec until unambiguous. Wittgenstein's argument is that this is structurally impossible. Examples carry shared form-of-life that prose cannot.
§ XI·Instruction · 2 of 4 11 / 19
XI Alignment requires public criteria Wittgenstein, P.I. § 243–2711953

"Be helpful" is not a specification.

The argument No "internal state" word has meaning without publicly checkable criteria of correctness. A purely private sensation, with no public criteria, would be a word that can't be used correctly or incorrectly — even by its inventor. Most system prompts are written in internal-state vocabulary: be helpful, be thoughtful, care about the user, be honest, be balanced. None has public criteria the model (or anyone) can check against. They are alignment vibes, not specs.
 Common sense

Internal-state instructions feel like instructing a person.

They produce vague, drifting behavior because the words don't denote anything checkable.

 The move

Rewrite alignment as publicly checkable behavior.

  • "Be helpful" → "When the request is task-shaped, respond with at least one specific actionable next step."
  • "Be honest" → "Cite a source for any factual claim. Label uncertainty explicitly."

XI · Wittgenstein II ← →
This single move — rewriting alignment in public-criteria form — takes most teams from RLHF-flavored vibes to actually testable specifications. Most engineers don't see that "be helpful" is structurally empty.
§ XII·Instruction · 3 of 4 12 / 19
XII Spec by paradigm + boundary Wittgenstein, P.I. § 65–711953

Most concepts have no essence. Spec by paradigm + boundary.

The argument Most natural-language concepts — game, chair, spam, toxic, helpful, harmful — have no necessary-and-sufficient defining conditions. They have overlapping family resemblances. There is no shared essence. Trying to define such concepts produces edges that fail: too narrow (misses cases) or too broad (catches innocents). Then come the exceptions. Then the swamp.
 Common sense

"Just write a clear definition."

Every spec, classifier, and content policy that tries to define collapses at the boundary. The definitional approach is the wrong shape for the problem.

 The move

Replace each definition with three sets.

  • Paradigm cases — clear yes.
  • Paradigm non-cases — clear no.
  • Boundary cases — with explicit rulings.

Calibrate against paradigms; debate only at the boundary.

XII · Wittgenstein III ← →
Ask: how is "spam" defined at your company? If the answer is a sentence, it's already broken. If it's "here are canonical yes/no examples plus rulings on close cases," that's family-resemblance spec, and it works.
§ XIII·Instruction · 4 of 4 13 / 19
XIII Tacit knowledge — the silent half Michael Polanyi, The Tacit Dimension1966

Your standards contain things you cannot articulate.

The argument "We can know more than we can tell." Every skilled person carries knowledge they cannot put into words — what a good PR looks like, when a system smells off, when a design is over-engineered. This is tacit knowledge. It transfers only through proximity, examples, and correction. It is structurally invisible to the prompt-writer at prompt-writing time.
 Common sense

"If we write the prompt well enough, the model will do exactly what we want."

Fails at the edges, because your standard contains tacit pieces you cannot articulate by definition. If you could, you already would have.

 The move

Budget for multi-turn iteration. The corrections are the training.

The model learns your tacit standard from your corrections across turns — not from the initial prompt. One-shot only works for tasks whose standard is fully articulable. Rare.

XIII · Polanyi ← →
Polanyi connects directly to Wittgenstein on rule-following. Same point, different vocabulary: full articulation isn't possible; shared background or correction is the missing carrier. The action point — budget for multi-turn — is the most universally useful in the talk.

§ XV·Part III · Judgment · 1 of 3 15 / 19
XV Is  ≠  ought · typical  ≠  good Hume (1739) · Moore (1903)is-ought · naturalistic fallacy

The model has no values. It has only averages.

Two arguments, one move Hume: you cannot derive an ought from an is. Moore: the typical and the natural are not the same as the good — that substitution is a category error. LLMs are pure is machines: statistical averages. Every "should" they output is laundered values — whichever ought had the strongest gradient signal in RLHF. "Best practices" they cite are modal practices, not best ones.
 Common sense

"Should I take this job?" "What's the best way to X?"

Treated as neutral analysis. They cannot be neutral; they cannot even be best. They are average values applied to average practice.

 The move

Name the value frame. Ask for excellent, not "best."

Normative: name the value frame in the prompt. Without it, you've outsourced ethics to RLHF averages.
"Best practice": ask for excellent contrasted with typical. Forces the model out of modal generation.

XV · Hume & Moore ← →
The slide that carries the value-laundering point engineers are most blind to. Explains both why LLMs feel "balanced" (RLHF averages) and why their advice is mediocre by default (modal not excellent).
§ XVI·Judgment · 2 of 3 16 / 19
XVI Phronēsis · the autonomy-vs-drift solution Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, Bk VI~ 340 BCE

There is no shortcut. Cultivate phronēsis.

The argument Phronēsis (practical wisdom) is the ability to make the right call in a particular situation when the rules don't fully tell you what to do. Aristotle's claim: it cannot be taught by lecture or rulebook. It develops only through repeated practice combined with feedback from someone who already has it.
 Common sense (two failure modes)

A: Confirm everything.  |  B: "You decide."

A — Micromanagement: doesn't scale. B — Full autonomy: causes drift; small misalignments compound. Neither works.

 The move

Graduated autonomy. Feedback as reasons, not corrections.

Agent decides. You sample. Feedback gives the principle, not the patch. Encode the principle into the next prompt. Eval-and-iterate loops are phronēsis cultivation.

XVI · Aristotle ← →
Resolves the most painful daily tension — the manager-engineer-AI triangle where everyone is over-checking or under-aligning. Aristotle's honest answer: there is no shortcut, but there is a method. Reasons, not corrections.
§ XVII·Judgment · 3 of 3 17 / 19
XVII When the measure becomes the target Charles Goodhart1975 · with roots in Wittgenstein

Every eval, optimized hard enough, becomes a lie.

The argument "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The instant a metric becomes the optimization target, behavior optimizes for the metric — not for what the metric was supposed to capture. RLHF is the canonical case: optimize for "human-preferred response," get sycophancy. Same mechanism as Wittgenstein's rule-following — metric underdetermines intent; optimization fills the gap.
 Common sense

"Just measure it precisely."

The precise measurement becomes the new target. Optimization gradually pulls behavior away from intent — including in ways you can't see, because the metric still looks good.

 The move

Rotate evals. Distrust long-running ones by default.

  • Don't make a single eval the target. Use multiple loosely-correlated checks.
  • Rotate evals. Hold some out. Audit by hand.
  • Assume any eval optimized hard for six months has been gamed.

XVII · Goodhart ← →
Tie back to Wittgenstein: Goodhart is a special case of rule-following. Eval underdetermines intent; optimization finds a satisfying continuation that drifts. Every eval is a 2-4-6-8 sequence — many continuations fit it.
§ XVIII·The Pattern 18 / 19

Meaning cannot be transmitted by words alone.

Every concept in this talk is a face of the same mistake. Common sense treats language and categories as naturally precise.

Almost every philosophical tradition that has survived 200 + years assumes the opposite — these things are inherently vague, value-laden, or context-dependent.

Engineering well means building structures that respect that, not ones that paper over it.

The action shape, across all ten ideas Invest in the things words can't carry: external grounding, public criteria, paradigm cases, named acts of speech, multi-turn correction, cultivated judgment, rotated evals.

Stop trying to make words do work that words structurally cannot do.
The Pattern One mistake · one fix
The through-line lands here. The audience should feel the slides snap into a single shape. The take-home is that all ten ideas manifest the same one mistake, and the same one fix.
